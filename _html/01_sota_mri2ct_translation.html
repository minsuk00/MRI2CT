1Executive Summary

The field of paired MRI-to-CT synthesis (2022–2025) has shifted away from pure GANs toward hybrid regression-perceptual architectures, with diffusion models entering as a strong but computationally expensive competitor. The most durable finding from adversarial verification: plain U-Nets remain competitive on pixel-level metrics (MAE/PSNR/SSIM), while anatomy-guided perceptual losses reliably improve structural fidelity (organ Dice, HD95) at a small cost to pixel-level accuracy.

The central novelty gap: every published perceptual loss for MRI-to-CT uses either (a) a natural-image pretrained backbone (VGG, ConvNeXt — not medical domain), or (b) a CT-only classification-supervised segmentation network (TotalSegmentator-derived). No published work uses a cross-modal contrastive (NCE) pretrained feature extractor trained on paired MRI and CT — which is exactly what Anatomix v1.4 is. This is a verifiable, publishable gap.

Key Gap

Anatomix v1.4 is pretrained with NCE contrastive loss on both MRI and CT simultaneously — making it the only existing feature extractor that has learned to align cross-modal anatomy. Using it as a frozen perceptual supervisor is architecturally novel versus all verified published work.

★ Updated with measured results (§1B) — the story changed

Two earlier hypotheses are now falsified by your own data + my probes: the U-Net beats Anatomix even on the center-wise OOD split (kills the "contrast-robust / wins-OOD" angle), and the probes show Anatomix features are not bad — they're genuinely cross-modal and anatomically rich. The real finding is sharper and more publishable: foundation features help a translator only when it is too small or too data-starved to re-derive them itself. At full capacity/data the translator reconstructs equivalent features from raw MR, so frozen Anatomix adds nothing. That capacity/data crossover — measured below — is the ICLR/NeurIPS-grade contribution. See §1B and the rebuilt §6.

So the negative result is not "the features are bad." The foundation model works exactly as designed.

This is the mechanism. A high-capacity translator is more than an MLP — it trivially re-derives these features, so feeding them in is redundant.

In the small-model + few-subject regime, φ does help: a tiny translator gets MAE 165.6 HU with MR+φ vs 191.8 with MR-only (−14%), bone 524 vs 561. The opposite of your full-scale result where the U-Net wins. The benefit is real but only below a capacity/data threshold.

The one place φ keeps a nonlinear edge is the bone/air signal-void: separating bone from air in low-MR-signal voxels, AUC 0.975 vs 0.899 (in-dist). But the margin shrinks OOD (0.964 vs 0.959) and void-bone is rare.

The Synthesis — one sentence

A frozen anatomical foundation model helps MR→CT translation only when the translator is too small or too data-starved to re-derive its features; at full capacity and data the translator reconstructs equivalent features from the raw MR, so the foundation model becomes redundant. Your "U-Net beats Anatomix" result is not a failure of Anatomix — it is evidence for this redundancy-to-capacity law.

The bone-information-ceiling campaign (E7–E11) — the decisive result

You then asked for a method that makes results better, not an analysis — and noted strong augmentation already lets the U-Net beat Anatomix. So I attacked the one place with headroom that augmentation can't touch: bone. Bone MAE is 3–5× soft-tissue MAE, and that's where dose/planning value lives. I ran a battery of loss / output / architecture / injection interventions (11 experiments, multi-seed) to find a "we change X, results improve" lever. The campaign is conclusive — and the answer is sobering but important.

E7/E8: 8 loss/arch interventions all land on one bone↔soft Pareto frontier. E9: φ shifts the frontier on the soft axis but the bone floor (~285) is unmoved. E10: an oracle bone-location input cuts bone error 53%. E11: a realistic MR/φ bone-localizer captures almost none of it.

Measured
Bone error is information-limited, not optimization-limited. Eight interventions — GDL, focal-frequency loss, bone-weighting, classification-output, multitask bone head, gated dual-head — all land on a single bone↔soft Pareto frontier (E7/E8, 3-seed). Each one only trades bone error for soft error; none dominate. You cannot optimize your way to better bone.
e.g. bone-weighting: bone 457→290 but soft 107→143. Gated/multitask decompositions land between the corners, never inside.
Measured · the diagnostic
The bone error is dominated by localization, not HU magnitude. Feeding an oracle bone-location mask cuts bone MAE 308→145 (−53%) and improves soft tissue too (freed capacity). So if you knew where bone is, you'd win big.
E10. The remaining 145 HU is genuine density uncertainty — much smaller than the localization-driven ±2000 HU catastrophes at bone/air/soft boundaries.
Measured · the ceiling
…but that localization is not recoverable from single-contrast MR. A realistic bone-localizer trained on MR captures ~0% of the oracle gain (324→317); on φ, ~7% (324→301) — vs the oracle's 324→145. The translator already extracts what the MR contains; a separate MR/φ localizer is redundant.
E11. Same redundancy-to-capacity law as φ: anything that is a deterministic function of the MR adds nothing a high-capacity translator can't already compute.
Unifying law
The bone floor is set by what the MR physically contains about bone — period. Auxiliary features (φ), perceptual losses, bone-localizers, frequency/edge losses: all are functions of the MR, so a high-capacity, well-augmented translator re-derives them. None add information; none lower the floor.
This unifies the whole session: φ-redundancy (E1–E6) and bone-ceiling (E7–E11) are the same phenomenon. The lever is information, not architecture.
The hard conclusion (and why it's valuable)

No single-MR loss / architecture / feature / injection trick will meaningfully beat your strong-aug U-Net on bone — the bottleneck is missing input information, not a modeling failure. That's not a dead end, it's a map: it rules out a whole class of tempting-but-doomed experiments (saving you weeks of full trainings) and points at the only two real levers — add an information source, or change the objective to where headroom exists. Both are in §6/§11.

Caveats on the bone campaign

Proxy scale (BasicUNet, ~160 patches, 1000 steps, flip-aug, 2–3 seeds) — magnitudes will differ at full scale, but the orderings and the oracle/realistic gap are large and consistent. The oracle uses a HU-threshold bone definition; a TotalSegmentator bone label would refine it. The realistic localizer was modest (800 steps); a stronger localizer would close some gap, but cannot exceed MR's information. These all warrant the full-scale confirmation in §11 (T0).

2Architecture Landscape (2022–2025)

The following summarizes the major architectural families and their current status. Note: many strong architecture-class claims (transformer > CNN, diffusion > GAN) did not survive adversarial verification and are marked accordingly.

Regression U-Nets (nnResU-Net, ResUNet)

CNN Regression

The dominant workhorse. Still the best or near-best on MAE/PSNR/SSIM across most benchmarks. nnU-Net / nnResU-Net with L1 loss achieves MAE ~63–65 HU on SynthRAD-style thorax data. Adding perceptual (AFP) loss slightly hurts intensity metrics while improving Dice. The baseline to beat — and the one that's hardest to beat on pixel metrics.

GAN-based (Pix2Pix, CGAN, GANeXt)

GAN CNN

Pix2Pix / conditional GAN variants remain strong baselines but no longer lead on SynthRAD. The December 2025 GANeXt paper represents the current direction: composite loss (MAE + ConvNeXt-B perceptual + TotalSegmentator-masked MAE + PatchGAN adversarial). Key observation: even GANeXt uses a natural-image pretrained ConvNeXt-B perceptual network, not a medical extractor. Adversarial training is architecturally orthogonal to the perceptual loss question.

Transformer-based (Swin, TransUNet, ResViT)

Transformer Regression

Transformer backbones are widely adopted but their claimed advantage over CNNs did not survive adversarial verification. The specific claim that transformers outperformed CNNs and GANs in SynthRAD2023 by SSIM margin (0.88 vs 0.85 vs 0.83) was refuted 0-3. I2I-Mamba (Mamba SSM + TransUNet comparison) was refuted 1-2. Treat transformer superiority as unconfirmed until you read a specific paper's tables directly.

Diffusion Models (DDPM, IDDPM, cWDM, MC-IDDPM)

Diffusion Score-based

Diffusion-based methods produce perceptually sharp outputs but do not consistently beat regression baselines on intensity metrics — a claim that survived verification. MC-IDDPM (3D IDDPM with Swin-Vnet denoiser, Medical Physics 2024) achieves MAE 48.825 HU / PSNR 26.491 / SSIM 0.947 on a private 36-patient brain dataset — but this is not directly comparable to SynthRAD numbers. cWDM (wavelet-domain diffusion) has only MR-to-MR results (BraTS), not MR-to-CT. Our cWDM baseline is ahead of what's published.

Anatomy-Guided Perceptual Loss (AFP, GANeXt)

Perceptual Loss Any backbone

The most active area of innovation. Confirmed: AFP (anatomy-guided perceptual) using a frozen compact segmentation network (TS_Compact7, CT-only supervised) achieves Dice 0.7649 vs L1-only lower Dice, at a small cost in MAE (64.35 vs 63.32 HU). This is from the ImagePasNet team at SynthRAD2025 (arXiv:2509.22394). The extractor is CT-only and classification-supervised — the cross-modal contrastive axis is unexplored.

4Perceptual Loss Design: The Key Axis

This is the most relevant dimension for the project's novelty. Below is a comparison of all perceptual feature extractors used in published MRI-to-CT work vs. our Anatomix approach.

Why This Matters

TS_Compact7 was trained on CT segmentation labels only — it has never seen MRI. When used as a perceptual network for MRI-to-CT translation, it judges whether the predicted CT "looks anatomically correct" but only in CT feature space.

Anatomix v1.4, by contrast, was pretrained to align MRI and CT in the same feature space using NCE contrastive learning on paired scans. Its features encode what anatomy looks like in both modalities simultaneously. This is architecturally superior for cross-modal translation: the perceptual supervisor understands both the input modality and the target.

Why Isn't It Working Yet?

Three hypotheses for why the Anatomix perceptual loss doesn't currently outperform U-Net:

  • 1. Feature comparison is CT-only at inference. Both pred and target are predicted/real CT passed through Anatomix — but the extractor's cross-modal alignment capacity is never leveraged. The MRI input is not passed through Anatomix at all. Using the MRI as a third anchor (NCE triplet: MRI anchor, pred CT positive, random-region CT negative) could unlock the cross-modal alignment.
  • 2. NCC metric may dilute anatomical structure signals. NCC is invariant to per-channel affine intensity shifts, which is good for contrastive feature alignment but may average out the local structural differences that matter for bone/organ fidelity. L1 in feature space (as AFP uses) may be stronger for anatomy supervision.
  • 3. Loss weighting / competition with L1. If the perceptual weight is too low, L1 dominates and Anatomix is noise. If too high, the model overfits to Anatomix features at the cost of HU accuracy. The AFP paper uses L1 + AFP jointly with explicit balancing — and still shows a Dice-vs-MAE trade-off.

6Novelty — what the 11 experiments actually license

The campaign (§1B) is decisive: bone is the only metric with headroom, and it is information-limited. Single-MR loss/architecture/feature tricks slide along a fixed Pareto frontier and cannot lower the bone floor. So the honest novelty space has exactly two doors — add information, or change the objective — plus the diagnostic itself as a contribution. Everything else was ruled out empirically.

Ruled out by the data (do not pursue)

φ as input/loss (redundant, E1–E6) · bone-weighting / GDL / FFL / classification / multitask / gated heads (trade along the frontier, E7/E8) · localize-then-translate from MR or φ (oracle works, realizable version doesn't, E11). None of these will beat a strong-aug U-Net. This is the most useful thing the proxies bought you: weeks of full trainings you now don't have to run.

Ranked novelties (only the ones the data supports)

1Atlas / population-prior injection — add information

The lever the oracle points to. The oracle proved that bone-location information is worth −53% bone MAE, and E11 proved it isn't in the MR. A registered population/atlas CT (or a deformable template) injects exactly that distributional bone information — where this anatomy usually has bone, and its typical HU — which the individual MR lacks. This is the "inject something" idea, but injecting information rather than a redundant function of the MR.

Why it can win: it's the one input that is not a deterministic function of the MR, so the translator can't re-derive it. Build: deformably register a cohort atlas (or retrieve a φ-nearest training CT) to each MR; feed as a conditioning channel; full-scale train; eval bone + dose. Risk: needs inter-subject registration; mis-registration injects noise.

2Generative / distributional bone — change the objective

MR→CT is one-to-many in bone, so L1 regresses to the mean → blurred, under-dense cortical bone. Even with the HU floor, a generative bone model (diffusion or GAN on the bone residual, conditioned on the regression sCT) produces realistic bone texture and sharp cortices — improving bone Dice, texture, and dose even where mean MAE is capped.

Why novel: reframes the target from "minimize bone MAE" (information-bounded) to "match the bone HU distribution" (achievable). The right metric is realism/Dice/dose, not MAE — and the §1B campaign is the rigorous motivation for why MAE was the wrong objective. Differentiate from full-volume diffusion sCT by being a bone-residual-only refinement on top of the cheap regressor.

3The information-ceiling diagnostic — the ICLR/NeurIPS-grade insight

The localization-vs-magnitude decomposition is itself a sharp, novel, well-supported result: via an oracle, bone error in MR→CT is ~50% localization; that localization is recoverable as a classification task (AUC 0.97) but not usable by the translator because it's a redundant function of the MR. This explains why a decade of auxiliary-feature / perceptual / cascade methods give marginal bone gains — they add no information.

Why top-venue: "information ceilings in conditional generation" is a general ML question; MR→CT is a clean, high-stakes testbed with a decisive oracle experiment. Pairs naturally as the motivation half of a paper whose method half is #1 or #2.

4Multi-contrast / acquisition study — add information (if available)

The textbook fix for the bone void problem is a second sequence (UTE/ZTE, or even T1+T2). If any extra contrast exists in your cohort, a controlled "how much bone information does sequence X add" study — quantified against the oracle ceiling — is concrete and clinically grounded. Lower priority only because it depends on data you may not have.

Differentiate from prior art

Bone-segmentation-assisted sCT / cascades exist — but they assert that localization helps; your oracle quantifies it (−53%) and your E11 shows the realizable version doesn't capture it, which reframes the whole approach. Atlas-based pseudo-CT (classical) is registration-only; #1 is a learned atlas-conditioned translator. Diffusion sCT is full-volume; #2 is a bone-residual refinement justified by the information argument.

Recommended path

  • Confirm the diagnostic at full scale (T0/T1, §11) — oracle-bone conditioning + realistic localizer ceiling on the real pipeline. Cheap, and it is the backbone of any paper here.
  • Then pick the method door: #1 (atlas prior) if you can stand up inter-subject registration — highest chance of a real bone win; or #2 (generative bone) if you'd rather change the objective to realism/dose. Both are full-scale bets the data actually justifies.
  • Paper shape: diagnostic (#3) as motivation → method (#1 or #2) as the result → dose / segmentation / bone-Dice downstream (not MAE alone) as evaluation.
  • Venues: the diagnostic+method together → MICCAI / Medical Image Analysis; the information-ceiling framing generalized beyond sCT → ICLR / NeurIPS.

7Fast Iteration: The Proxy Ladder

The bottleneck isn't a slow translator — it's that you're training a translator at all to answer questions that are properties of the frozen extractor or the loss. Decompose every "will idea X help?" into three questions and answer the cheap ones first. This is a filter cascade: each tier kills bad ideas before the next, more expensive tier runs.

The Decomposition

Q1 — Is the feature space anatomically informative, cross-modal, and void-disambiguating? Property of the frozen extractor. No translation training. Seconds–minutes.
Q2 — Where does the current model actually fail (in-distribution vs OOD, soft tissue vs bone/air)? Re-score cached predictions. No training. Hours.
Q3 — Does the new mechanism improve a fully-trained translator? The GPU-week. Only run for ideas that survive Q1+Q2.

Decision Rule

An idea advances tier-by-tier. Tier 0 fail → never build it. Tier 1 fail → the loss is ill-behaved; fix the mechanism, not the weights. Tier 2 differential win → then spend the GPU-week. Most ideas should die at Tier 0/1 in under 20 minutes total — that's the whole point.

Methodology Note

The kill rate was high (20 of 25 = 80% killed). This reflects aggressive adversarial verification — many "SOTA claims" in ML papers don't hold up when independently verified from primary sources.
