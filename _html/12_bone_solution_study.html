Solving and bounding the MR→CT bone problem

Question. Reports 10/11 showed every MR→CT model undershoots and blurs cortical bone, and argued the error is information-limited (the single MR cannot encode cortical HU), not fixable by loss/architecture tweaks. This study tests that claim and searches for what, if anything, helps, via (1) a controlled toy where the MR's bone information is a dial, (2) a diverse idea bake-off on the toy, and (3) a model-free measurement on the real SynthRAD data. Generated by bone_study/.

Verdict.
  • Bone HU is information-limited, confirmed three independent ways. On real data, a privileged baseline that already knows where every bone voxel is and just predicts the population-mean bone HU (zero MR used) scores 324 HU on skull and 155 HU on thorax. The trained U-Net, using the full MR, scores 242 and 162: it beats that baseline by only ~25% on skull and on thorax ribs/spine fails to beat it at all (162 vs 155, slightly worse). This is not an OOD artifact: an in-distribution control (donors and queries both center A, no domain shift) still gives bone retrieval only ~9% below the prior with a large irreducible spread. So the MR contributes almost nothing to per-voxel bone HU beyond locating the bone. In the toy, when the MR carries no bone-intensity information (alpha=0) no objective (L1/L2/bone-weighted/diffusion) beats the floor.
  • The user's retrieval idea cannot fix intensity by itself. Retrieval-by-MR (kNN) on real data scores 300 HU, worse than the U-Net, because the retrieval key is the same information-limited MR. Retrieval only helps to the extent the donor carries bone-HU information the MR lacks, which it does not get from MR-similarity.
  • But the error has two separable halves. The intensity half is information-limited (a hard ceiling); the blur half is spatial and is fixable. A conditional diffusion sample and a sharp registered template both restore edge sharpness to ~real-CT, even on the ceiling.
  • The only lever that moves the intensity ceiling is genuine side-information the MR lacks (an oracle bone-HU template is perfect in the toy). Practical routes: bone-sensitive MR sequences (UTE/ZTE), or an atlas/retrieval prior carrying population bone HU+shape (modest gain, mostly shape).

1. The toy model

A 1D signal stands in for a scan line. The CT is a smooth soft-tissue field plus rare, sharp, tall bone peaks (right-skewed heights, so dense cortical bone is the rare high tail). The MR tracks soft tissue well and marks each bone with a void dip (presence + jittered location), but its encoding of bone height is controlled by a dial:

alpha_info in [0,1]: fraction of true bone height the MR encodes (1 = recoverable / objective-limited, 0 = absent / information-limited).
jitter: std (voxels) of bone-edge location uncertainty in the MR (the source of blur).
CT (blue) = soft tissue + sharp bones; MR (orange) = soft tissue + void dips. At alpha=1 the void depth tracks bone height; at alpha=0 it does not.

By construction the void depth correlates with true height by |r|≈0 at alpha=0 and ≈0.6 at alpha=1 (a realistic, imperfect ceiling). One caveat on the axis: a held-out probe (predict height from the MR) shows recoverable bone-height info jumps 0 → ~0.34 R² between alpha=0 and alpha=0.25, then saturates (~0.38 by alpha=1). So alpha cleanly separates info-absent (alpha=0) from info-present (alpha>0), the contrast the conclusions rest on, but it is not a linearly-calibrated 'fraction of information'; treat intermediate-alpha points as info-present, not as a graded information ramp.

4. Retrieval / template prior (the proposed idea), tested honestly

We model a retrieved + registered donor CT as an extra template channel with two independent quality axes mirroring reality: intensity fidelity (can the retrieval key find a donor with the right bone HU? bounded by the MR) and registration fidelity (residual bone-edge misalignment).

Two construction choices, stated for honesty: (1) the retrieved donor height uses the same formula as the MR's own marker (independent noise), i.e. a second redundant readout of MR-accessible info, which is the intended model of 'retrieval key = the MR' and is why retrieved improves with alpha (variance reduction of an already-present signal, not new information). (2) trueH_misreg deliberately uses worse registration (jitter 2.5 vs 0.8), so it does not isolate one axis; it shows true-HU content survives bad registration. (3) oracle equals the target by construction (perfect soft + true bone HU/location); it is the unreachable upper bound, never evidence that retrieval works.

Metric note: edge_sharp is a ratio of max local gradients (clipped at 2), so it credits gradient magnitude and a noisy prediction could in principle inflate it. Here the sharpness claim is also backed by the qualitative overlay (the samples are visibly clean sharp bones, not noise) and by the paired bone_mae, which correctly penalizes the sample's positional error.
Important caveat (real vs toy). This toy is a pixel-space DDPM under ideal conditions, so its samples reach real-CT edge sharpness. On the real data, report 11 found the existing generative baselines (MC-DDPM, cWDM, MAISI) still blur bone (edge sharpness ~0.79-0.82, no better than the U-Net's 0.81; the one model at 0.87 is koalAI, a regression baseline, not diffusion), because they operate through lossy latent/wavelet bottlenecks (the MAISI VAE skull bleed is the clearest case). So 'a generative head restores bone sharpness' is demonstrated in principle here but is not delivered by the current real models: realizing it needs a pixel-space or high-frequency-preserving design, not an off-the-shelf latent diffusion.

6. Synthesis: one information ceiling

Sweeping alpha_info shows four behaviours. The regressor is blur-capped (flat ~1.0 in alpha): at jitter=1.5 its per-voxel bone error is dominated by edge blur, so more intensity information alone barely helps. Realistic retrieval improves with MR info (it can rescale its template) but plateaus far above the oracle. Only the oracle template (true side-information) reaches both low MAE and real-CT edges. The diffusion sample is highest on MAE (it optimizes realism, not MAE, and MAE rewards blur) yet leads on edge sharpness at every alpha. The intensity/blur split in one figure: per-voxel HU accuracy is information-capped, sharpness is not.

7. Real-data confirmation: the bone information floor

Key fact: any MR embedding is a deterministic function of the MR, so no MR-based predictor (U-Net, retrieval, diffusion) can carry more bone information than the MR itself. We estimate that ceiling model-free on real SynthRAD center-wise data (donors = train center-A subjects; queries = the U-Net's own OOD val set, center C for head&neck and center B for thorax). Baselines, all evaluated at the same bone voxels:

  • no-info prior: knows the bone mask and predicts the population-mean bone HU, using zero MR. A privileged lower bound on 'use no MR information'.
  • retrieval (kNN, MR): k=16 nearest neighbors in local-MR-patch space (= retrieval by MR appearance), prediction = neighbor-mean CT HU. A model-free MR predictor.
  • U-Net sCT: the trained model, the strong learned MR predictor.
  • neighbor spread: within-MR-neighborhood CT-HU std, an estimate of the irreducible floor (loose, since raw-patch kNN is a weak metric).

For bone, the U-Net is at the information floor: it improves only ~25% over the privileged no-MR prior on skull and on thorax fails to beat it (162 vs 155 HU, slightly worse), with retrieval-by-MR no better. For soft tissue the picture flips on thorax: the U-Net clearly beats the prior (41 vs 62 HU), the positive control showing the MR does carry soft-tissue information. This is the real-data analogue of the toy's alpha≈0 (bone) vs alpha>0 (soft) regimes. Read the ceiling off the U-Net-vs-prior gap, not the raw-patch kNN: the kNN is a weak predictor (it is worse than the prior even for soft tissue), so it corroborates direction but its absolute MAE and the neighbor-spread are loose, not tight floors.

Is this an OOD artifact? No, an in-distribution control says it is intrinsic.

The fairest objection: donors are center A and queries are a different center, so 'U-Net fails to beat the prior' could be the U-Net being out-of-distribution rather than the MR being uninformative. Two defenses. (1) The prior eats the same center shift (it is the donor center-A bone mean scored on the query center), yet the MR-using U-Net still does not beat it, so whatever OOD penalty exists hits both and the MR adds nothing on top. (2) A direct in-distribution control, donors AND queries both center A (no domain shift):

Bone HU is largely MR-unpredictable without any domain shift, so the information limit is intrinsic, not OOD. (Soft retrieval is worse than its prior here too, confirming the kNN is just a weak predictor; the soft information lives in the MR but this particular estimator does not extract it, which is why the learned U-Net, not the kNN, is the soft positive control.)

Robustness caveats (honest limits of this real-data section). (a) Single seed, 12 donors / 6 query subjects per region; voxels within a subject are correlated so the effective n is ~subjects, not voxels, and the ~25% / ~0% figures are point estimates without per-subject CIs. (b) The 'neighbor spread' is a loose upper estimate of the floor (raw-patch L2 kNN is a weak metric), not a tight irreducible bound. (c) On head&neck the U-Net soft MAE (75) is itself worse than the prior (60), an OOD soft-tissue offset on center C; the clean soft positive control is thorax. None of these flips the bone verdict, which is corroborated by the in-distribution control, the toy mechanism, and report 11's six-model convergence, but the precise percentages should be read as indicative.

8. What to actually do

Stop: loss reweighting, bone-Dice tuning, removing the sigmoid cap, post-hoc HU gain. Proven not to move the per-voxel bone ceiling (here and in report 11's 6-model convergence).

To raise the intensity ceiling (the hard half), inject information the MR lacks:

  • Bone-sensitive MR (UTE/ZTE/dual-echo) directly raises alpha; the cleanest fix where the sequence is available (SynthRAD is single T1/T2, so likely not, but worth confirming per cohort).
  • Atlas / retrieval prior carrying population bone HU + shape as an extra input. Expect a modest per-voxel gain (toy: a sharp located template helps mostly shape/blur, intensity stays bounded), and beware misregistration. Worth it primarily for the shape/sharpness it contributes, not as an intensity cure.

To fix the blur / realism half (cheaper, and clinically real for DRR / bone visualization):

  • Generative head (diffusion or adversarial high-frequency term): in principle restores cortical edge sharpness even at the information ceiling. But it must be pixel-space or HF-preserving: report 11's existing latent/wavelet diffusion baselines do not (they blur bone like the U-Net). Best ROI if downstream cares about bone appearance (DRR) rather than exact per-voxel HU, and only with the right (non-lossy-bottleneck) architecture.

Reframe the objective/eval (connects to the decision-aware direction): bone HU is irreducibly uncertain from MR, so predict calibrated uncertainty on bone and evaluate on the downstream decision (dose / DRR), not pixel HU. The soft-tissue / PSNR story is separate and already near optimal; the cheap PSNR win there is the tight body mask (report 10), not modeling. The concrete recipe for this is Section 9.

9. The recommended path: decision-aware synthesis with calibrated uncertainty

Status: proposed path, not a demonstrated result. Sections 1-7 and the appendices are experiments with proof. This section is prescriptive: given the proven ceiling, it is what we recommend building, not something this study has already shown to work.

The proven boundaries: patient-specific cortical HU is unrecoverable from SynthRAD's single conventional MR by any model (toy + information floor + the real atlas experiment), retrieval is ruled out (Appendix C), and the only true fixes (bone-sensitive UTE/ZTE MR, or a same-patient prior CT) are either unavailable or circular for this project. So the goal shifts from 'make bone correct' (impossible) to 'make the model honest about bone, and find out whether the bone error even changes the clinical decision'.

Step 0 (do first): does the bone error change the dose?

Run the existing sCT vs the real CT through the dose pipeline (pyRadPlan): DVH, gamma. Two outcomes, both publishable. Dose robust to the bone error → the bone 'problem' is largely a metric artifact and the U-Net is already clinically sufficient. Dose sensitive → you have justified the calibrated-uncertainty objective below with evidence. This is the cheapest, highest-information next step; everything else is conditional on it.

Calibrated uncertainty: the recipe

Output two co-registered channels: channel 0 = μ (the sCT, unchanged deliverable), channel 1 = b (per-voxel uncertainty, HU units). Concretely:

  • Head: final conv output_nc 1→2; sigmoid on μ only, raw log b for the scale.
  • Loss: replace L1 with the matching Laplace NLL mean(|y-μ|/b + log b), b=exp(log b). The net inflates b exactly where it cannot predict (bone) for free. (Stabilise with an L1 warm-up on μ or β-NLL, or b can run away early.)
  • Calibrate post-hoc on a held-out set (the raw NLL scale is almost always off): a scalar multiplier or isotonic regression on the predictive CDF, fit separately for bone and soft.
  • Verify with a bone-restricted reliability diagram (empirical vs nominal coverage), plus PICP (coverage) and MPIW (interval width). Bone intervals should be wide and well-covered; soft narrow and well-covered.
  • Distribution-free options: quantile regression (pinball loss, predict 5/50/95 percentiles), or wrap any of the above in split-conformal prediction for finite-sample coverage guarantees. Deep ensembles of NLL heads calibrate best but cost K× training.

What the uncertainty is for (it does not fix bone, it makes the error honest and actionable)

  • Propagate to dose (main use): sample N sCTs (μ + Laplace(b)) or run μ ± k·b scenarios through pyRadPlan → a DVH / dose-uncertainty band. Tight band = bone error does not matter for the plan; wide band = you have quantified exactly what the bone limit costs. This is the quantitative form of Step 0.
  • Flag / QA / OOD: the uncertainty map routes high-uncertainty regions and cases to human review or to real-CT acquisition; abnormal anatomy lights up the whole map. The model says 'I do not know this bone' instead of confidently undershooting it.
  • Robust / gated planning: worst-case-robust optimisation over the uncertainty; auto-approve only plans whose uncertainty-driven dose error is under tolerance.
  • For the paper: the calibrated map localises and quantifies the information limit this study proved, and puts honest error bars on the dose.
On the generative head (demoted). A pixel-space generative head can make bone look sharp and population-realistic, but: it does not make bone correct (information limit), it worsens per-voxel HU, and the existing real latent/wavelet diffusion baselines did not even deliver the sharpness (Section 5 caveat; Appendix C showed a registered real CT is blurrier than the U-Net at the bone boundary). So pursue it only if a downstream consumer specifically needs bone appearance (a human-reviewed DRR), or if Step 0 shows the dose is sensitive to bone density (where a realistic-density bone may dose better than a blurred-undershot one). Do not lead with it.

Appendix C: does retrieval actually help bone? The real atlas experiment

The analysis above argues retrieval supplies the population prior, not patient-specific cortical HU. A reasonable objection: a retrieved real CT, deformed onto the patient, carries real bone structure, so maybe it helps the blur half even if not the intensity half. We tested this directly by building the actual atlas pipeline on real SynthRAD head&neck data (n=10 query subjects): retrieve donors, deformably register them (ANTs SyN), fuse the top-3 warped donor CTs, and measure what they deliver on bone vs the U-Net and a no-MR prior. Two arms:

  • realistic: donors = center-A train, retrieved by MR similarity, registered MR→MR (the deployable pipeline).
  • oracle: donors = same-center, retrieved by CT similarity and registered CT→CT (best-possible donor + alignment, no domain shift). Unreachable in practice because it uses the query's own CT, so it is a strict upper bound.

Decisive add-info test (leave-one-query-out, bootstrap CI)

Does the atlas add bone-HU information beyond the U-Net? Leave-one-query-out, fit true_HU on [U-Net] vs [U-Net, atlas], compare held-out bone MAE:

fit[U-Net] = 233.7 HU. realistic atlas adds +2.4 HU (95% CI [-1.4, +6.7]) → not significant (the CI crosses 0; the most it could add is ~7 HU, <3% of the 234 HU floor). oracle atlas adds +11.6 HU (95% CI [+6.3, +17.2]) → significant but only ~5% of the floor, and it cheats with the query CT.
Conclusion (real pipeline, proven). The U-Net dominates the atlas: it is more accurate than every atlas configuration (bone MAE 233 vs realistic 312 / oracle 265; the realistic atlas is worse than even a constant median prediction, 294) and sharper than the usable (fused) atlas. The deployable MR-driven retrieval adds no significant bone-HU information beyond the U-Net. Even the oracle, which cheats by retrieving and aligning on the query's own CT, adds only ~5% of the floor and as a standalone prediction is still worse than the U-Net. In 0 of 10 queries did any atlas beat the U-Net on intensity and sharpness together.

The honest nuance (partly vindicating the idea). A single aligned donor genuinely is sharp: its raw bone-edge gradient (0.83) exceeds the U-Net's (0.74), because it is real CT. But that single donor carries 320-368 HU of bone error (worse than guessing a constant), so its sharpness is unusable. Multi-atlas fusion is what lowers the HU error (to 265-312), and fusion averages misaligned donors, which blurs the edges back below the U-Net (0.60-0.69). So the atlas offers sharp-or-accurate, never both, and neither point on that tradeoff beats the U-Net. The killer is cross-subject cortical registration: a thin skull rim shifted 1-2 voxels is a large per-voxel error, which is exactly why atlas-based sCT was historically overtaken by CNNs. Note the registration resolution is not the limiter (bone MAE at 1.5mm vs 3mm registration was 282 vs 284).

Scope. head&neck skull, n=10 queries, K=3 fusion, single seed. The intensity conclusion (no usable gain, even oracle) is robust and consistent with the toy and the information floor; the n=10 CI cannot exclude a <7 HU realistic gain, so the claim is 'no usable gain', not 'exactly zero'. A generative head remains a separate, non-registration lever for sharpness (Section 5); this experiment only rules out the retrieve-and-deform route.
