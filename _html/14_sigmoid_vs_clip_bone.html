Sigmoid or clipping? Why the U-Net renders bone as flat gray

Question

The MR->CT U-Net renders bone as a flat, uniform mid-gray: no contrast between the dense cortical shell and the lower-density trabecular interior. Switching the final layer from sigmoid (output mapped to HU [-1024, 1024], target clipped at +/-1024) to linear (target clipped at [-1024, 3000]) makes the bone show visible cortical-vs-interior structure and "look much better."

Two things change at once between those two models: the output activation (sigmoid -> linear) and the target clip range (1024 -> 3000). The question: which one actually causes the flat bone, and what should we change? A prior series of reports (docs 9-13) concluded bone error is information-limited (the MR does not carry bone density); the user suspected that conclusion was contaminated by an optimization/parameterization artifact of the sigmoid.

It is the clipping, not the sigmoid. In a controlled toy where the input provably contains the bone information, widening the target clip from 1024 to 3000 recovers cortical-vs-interior contrast almost perfectly (separation 436 -> 1520 HU vs a ground-truth 1533), while the activation barely moves anything (sigmoid ~= linear at every clip). The decisive cell -- sigmoid + clip 3000 -- fully recovers the structure (within-bone correlation 0.997) and is in fact the best of the four, because the bounded sigmoid avoids the linear head's overshoot. So: widen the CT clip range; you can keep the sigmoid. A separate test rules out the vanishing-gradient worry: under Adam the sigmoid reaches dense bone and air to within ~50 HU, so rail saturation does not bite. But this is a representation/dynamic-range fix, not a refutation of docs 9-13: on real data, within a fixed shell depth, bone density still scatters +/-300 HU that the MR cannot resolve. Confirmed in the real unet_baseline training pipeline (not just the toy): an equal-budget 2x2 splits by clip, not activation -- holding the sigmoid fixed and widening the clip cuts bone MAE 61%, and at the wide clip the sigmoid out-performs the linear head on bone. The old reports conflated a fixable clipping artifact with the genuine information limit; both are real.

The toy

To separate "the sigmoid cannot express bone" from "the MR has no bone info," we must remove the information question entirely. So we built a synthetic 2-D phantom where the input provably determines the bone:

  • Ground-truth CT phantom: a soft-tissue body containing several bones, each

with a bright cortical shell (HU drawn per-bone in 1400-2800) around a darker trabecular interior (300-900), plus air. The cortical/trabecular labels are known exactly (and the bones are placed disjoint, so the labels never mix).

  • pseudo-MR input: an invertible, monotonic compression of the CT,

mr = asinh(ct/400)/3 + noise(0.01). A prove-it check confirmed cortical and trabecular bands stay separable at 13.9x the noise level (0.0 classification error over 200k samples). So the information to reconstruct bone -- including cortical-vs-interior -- is guaranteed present in the input.

Because the info is present, any failure to render bone contrast is purely representational, which is exactly the sigmoid-vs-clip question. We then run a 2x2: activation {sigmoid, linear} x target-clip {1024, 3000}, training an identical tiny U-Net (Adam, L1 loss in fixed HU units, BatchNorm -- same recipe as the real trainer) on each cell, three seeds, on a fixed held-out eval set.

Crucially the sigmoid's output range widens in lockstep with the clip (sigmoid

  • clip3000 maps to [-1024, 3000], not a strawman capped at 1024) -- verified in the

prove-it pass.

Hypotheses & debate

Four subagents each steelmanned one hypothesis and committed to a falsifiable prediction for the 2x2:

  • H1 sigmoid / vanishing gradients -- the saturating sigmoid throttles

gradients into the dense-bone regime; the activation is the main effect. Decisive falsifier: if sigmoid + clip3000 recovers contrast, H1 is dead.

  • H2 target clipping -- clipping at 1024 erases cortical structure from the

supervision (every voxel >1024 is clamped to one value); the clip is the main effect, activation is a red herring. Clean discriminator: predicted-HU spread among true >1024 voxels (~0 at clip1024, large at clip3000, both heads).

  • H3 saturation-coupling (interaction) -- narrow clip pushes bone into the

sigmoid's low-slope tail; both off-diagonal cells recover, with a sub-additive interaction term.

  • H4 info-limit intact -- the methodological guard: because the toy has

info, it cannot by itself refute docs 9-13; the real adjudicator is the within-shell-depth density spread on real data.

The debate converged on the right experiment and one warning (H4): the toy answers only the representation question; the real-data within-shell test answers the information question. We ran both.

But the information floor is real (the docs 9-13 caveat survives)

The H4 guard: does the wider clip recover real density information, or just give the geometric shell prior (outer shell = cortical) room to draw? Bone location and shell depth ARE visible in MR; per-voxel density at a fixed depth may not be. We test this model-free on real GT: bin bone voxels by distance-to-surface, and ask how much true HU still varies within a fixed depth.

Within a fixed shell depth, real bone density still scatters +/-300 HU. Thehigh

between-depth trend (mean HU rising 470 -> 824 with depth) is the geometric shell prior -- recoverable from MR, and what makes the wider-clip model "look better." The within-depth +/-300 HU is not resolvable from geometry, matching doc 13's model-free 222 HU k-NN floor. So widening the clip recovers the determined part (the >1024 ceiling) and the shell gradient, but not the residual per-voxel density.

Does the sigmoid undershoot the extremes? (the vanishing-gradient test)

A fair objection: maybe the sigmoid is fine in the 2x2 above only because clip3000 moved bone off the saturated rail; the classic worry is that a sigmoid is hard to train for the extremes (dense bone at the upper rail, air at the lower rail) because sigma'(z) -> 0 there starves the gradient. We tested this directly, with NO clip confound: output range set wider than the data ([-1100, 3100]), info present, and we vary activation {sigmoid, linear} x optimizer {Adam, SGD+momentum}, plus a sigmoid+Adam cell with a tight range ([-1000, 2800]) so air and max-cortical sit exactly at the rails.

The vanishing-gradient mechanism is real (sigma'->0 at the rails) but Adam rescales the tiny gradient back up, so it does not bite. Even when targets sit exactly at the sigmoid's rail (tight range) it still reaches them, because out=1e-4 already maps to -999.6 HU, inside L1 tolerance.

If anything the bounded sigmoid is the more robust head. Under plain SGD the linearmedium

head destabilized and collapsed to a constant (corr nan), while the bounded sigmoid stayed stable and trained (corr 0.994). (SGD's LR was not tuned per-head, so the linear collapse is partly a high-LR instability -- but the takeaway holds: the sigmoid did not fail at the extremes under either optimizer.)

So the real model's flat bone is not the sigmoid failing to reach high HU (it reaches 2800+ fine). It is the clip erasing the >1024 target plus the information limit averaging ambiguous voxels to the conditional mean.

Confirmation at scale

The toy is the causal instrument (info held present, one variable at a time); the real-data analyses confirm both halves of its conclusion on actual SynthRAD CT: the clip ceiling erases 11.8% of real bone (Fig 3 left, table), and the information floor persists at +/-300 HU within any shell (Fig 3 right). The sigmoid model's 1024 cap is a mathematical fact of pred*2048-1024, so its flat cortical bone is guaranteed, not incidental.

The real training pipeline: a 2x2 in the actual unet_baseline setup

To rule out any "the toy isn't your real setup" objection, we ran the 2x2 in the project's OWN pipeline: real common/data.py transforms, the real 3D Unet (num_downs 4, ngf 16, batchnorm), real 128^3 patches + augmentation, Adam 3e-4 + grad-clip 1.0, real center-wise split, evaluated on held-out brain subjects via the same sliding-window inference. Only the final activation {sigmoid, linear} and clip {1024, 3000} vary; same seed, same data, equal budget (1500 steps). This is an equal-budget parity + clip-effect test, not a full-convergence run (the production model trains ~500k steps; bone is the slowest thing to learn), so absolute bone is weak -- but the comparison is what we are after.

Bounds of this run: one seed (the small clip1024 soft-tissue edge for the linear head is within run-to-run noise); 1500 steps so absolute bone is far from the production model's quality (this measures parity + the clip effect, not final bone); and cross-clip body MAE is confounded by the normalized-loss scale (soft tissue is down-weighted at clip3000, which is why its body MAE looks worse -- not a regression). The clean reads -- same-clip activation comparison and the within-sigmoid clip effect -- are unaffected. A 2-3 seed rerun would pin down the small clip1024 difference but does not change the direction.

What we did not run: a direct linear-vs-sigmoid comparison of the user's own two real checkpoints (the linear checkpoint path wasn't available this session). The toy + model-free real-data facts establish the mechanism conclusively, but the single most direct confirmation would be to re-evaluate the user's linear model with the within-shell-depth test of Fig 3 -- prediction: it improves the >1024 cortical voxels and the dynamic range, but its within-shell-depth correlation with GT is no better than the sigmoid model's (the residual is information-limited).

Conclusion & confidence

Proven (high confidence):

  • The flat bone is caused by the +/-1024 target clip, not the sigmoid. In a

controlled, info-present toy the result splits by clip, not activation, across 3 seeds; sigmoid + clip3000 recovers full cortical contrast (corr 0.997).

  • Keep the sigmoid, widen the clip. The bounded sigmoid recovers the structure

AND avoids the linear head's overshoot; it is the best of the four cells.

  • Vanishing gradients do not bite under Adam. The sigmoid reaches both bone (~2800)

and air (-1000) extremes to within ~50 HU (corr 0.998), no worse than linear; the sigma'->0 rail effect is real but rescaled away by Adam. Under SGD the linear head was the one that collapsed, not the sigmoid.

  • On real data the clip erases 11.8% of bone (genuine, recoverable cortical HU),

and -- separately -- a +/-300 HU within-shell density floor remains information-limited, confirming docs 9-13 for that component.

  • Confirmed in the real unet_baseline pipeline (not just the toy): an

equal-budget 2x2 splits by clip, not activation; holding the sigmoid fixed and widening the clip cuts bone MAE 61% (967->374) and lifts predicted bone max 93->1177 HU, and at clip3000 the sigmoid out-performs the linear head on bone.

What to do, concretely:

  1. Change the CT normalization clip from [-1024, 1024] to [-1024, 3000] (covers

the observed cortical max ~3071 HU) in the data pipeline and trainers.

  1. Keep the sigmoid head, rescaled to the new range (pred = sigmoid()*4024 - 1024

at inference). No need to switch to a linear head -- it only adds overshoot.

  1. Expect a real qualitative bone improvement and lower full-HU bone MAE on the

cortical (>1024) voxels; do not expect the within-shell density floor to move (and note doc 09: the clipped PSNR metric may barely register the gain -- evaluate in full-HU bone MAE).

The reconciliation: the user is right that part of the old "bone failure" was a fixable representation artifact (the clip), which docs 9-13 conflated with the information limit. Both are real and now separated: widen the clip to fix the representable cortical ceiling; the residual within-shell density remains the genuine MR information limit.

Expected, not proven: the direct within-shell test on the user's own linear checkpoint (see caveat).
