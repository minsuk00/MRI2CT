# Solving and bounding the MR->CT bone problem

**Source HTML:** _html/12_bone_solution_study.html
**Date:** undated (reports 10/11 context; series 2026-06)
**TL;DR:** Bone HU is information-limited, confirmed three ways (toy alpha-dial, idea bake-off, real-data model-free floor): the single MR cannot encode cortical HU, so no loss/architecture/retrieval trick beats the ceiling. The error splits into an intensity half (hard ceiling) and a blur half (fixable by a pixel-space generative head). Only genuine side-information the MR lacks (UTE/ZTE sequence, oracle bone template) moves the intensity ceiling.

**Question.** Reports 10/11 showed every MR->CT model undershoots and blurs cortical bone, and argued the error is information-limited (the single MR cannot encode cortical HU), not fixable by loss/architecture tweaks. This study tests that claim and searches for what helps via (1) a controlled toy where the MR's bone information is a dial, (2) a diverse idea bake-off on the toy, and (3) a model-free measurement on real SynthRAD data. Generated by `bone_study/`.

## Verdict

- **Bone HU is information-limited, confirmed three independent ways.** On real data, a privileged baseline that already knows where every bone voxel is and predicts the population-mean bone HU (zero MR used) scores 324 HU on skull and 155 HU on thorax. The trained U-Net, using the full MR, scores 242 and 162: beats that baseline only ~25% on skull and on thorax ribs/spine fails to beat it at all (162 vs 155, slightly worse). Not an OOD artifact: an in-distribution control (donors and queries both center A, no domain shift) still gives bone retrieval only ~9% below the prior with a large irreducible spread. In the toy, when the MR carries no bone-intensity info (alpha=0) no objective (L1/L2/bone-weighted/diffusion) beats the floor.
- **The retrieval idea cannot fix intensity by itself.** Retrieval-by-MR (kNN) on real data scores 300 HU, worse than the U-Net, because the retrieval key is the same information-limited MR. Retrieval only helps to the extent the donor carries bone-HU info the MR lacks, which it does not get from MR-similarity.
- **The error has two separable halves.** The intensity half is information-limited (hard ceiling); the blur half is spatial and fixable. A conditional diffusion sample and a sharp registered template both restore edge sharpness to ~real-CT, even on the ceiling.
- **The only lever that moves the intensity ceiling is genuine side-information the MR lacks** (an oracle bone-HU template is perfect in the toy). Practical routes: bone-sensitive MR sequences (UTE/ZTE), or an atlas/retrieval prior carrying population bone HU+shape (modest gain, mostly shape).

## 1. The toy model

A 1D signal stands in for a scan line. The CT is a smooth soft-tissue field plus rare, sharp, tall bone peaks (right-skewed heights, so dense cortical bone is the rare high tail). The MR tracks soft tissue well and marks each bone with a void dip (presence + jittered location), but its encoding of bone height is controlled by a dial:

- `alpha_info` in [0,1]: fraction of true bone height the MR encodes (1 = recoverable / objective-limited, 0 = absent / information-limited).
- `jitter`: std (voxels) of bone-edge location uncertainty in the MR (the source of blur).

By construction the void depth correlates with true height by |r|~0 at alpha=0 and ~0.6 at alpha=1 (a realistic, imperfect ceiling). Axis caveat: a held-out probe (predict height from the MR) shows recoverable bone-height info jumps 0 -> ~0.34 R^2 between alpha=0 and alpha=0.25, then saturates (~0.38 by alpha=1). So alpha cleanly separates info-absent (alpha=0) from info-present (alpha>0), the contrast the conclusions rest on, but it is not a linearly-calibrated "fraction of information"; treat intermediate-alpha points as info-present, not as a graded ramp.

## 2. The regressor reproduces undershoot + blur

An L1 CNN regressor (the U-Net stand-in) across regimes:

| variant | amp bias | amp scatter | edge sharp | bone MAE | soft MAE |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| alpha=0.0, jitter=0.0 | -0.11 | 0.52 | 0.79 | 0.878 | 0.031 |
| alpha=0.0, jitter=1.5 | -0.28 | 0.61 | 0.85 | 1.049 | 0.045 |
| alpha=0.5, jitter=1.5 | -0.31 | 0.51 | 0.85 | 0.988 | 0.046 |
| alpha=1.0, jitter=0.0 | -0.13 | 0.40 | 0.80 | 0.804 | 0.031 |
| alpha=1.0, jitter=1.5 | -0.32 | 0.52 | 0.84 | 0.999 | 0.047 |

amp bias <0 = undershoot; edge sharp 1.0 = real-CT-sharp; lower MAE better.

alpha controls recoverable intensity (amp scatter collapses as alpha rises); jitter sets the blur floor (edge sharp < 1 and a negative amp bias appear once jitter>0). The two failure modes the reports describe are one mechanism, split by spatial scale.

## 3. Cheap objective tweaks do not solve it

The moves argued to be dead ends (loss swaps, bone weighting, post-hoc recalibration), at the information-limited regime (alpha=0, jitter=1.5):

| variant | amp bias | amp scatter | edge sharp | bone MAE | soft MAE |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| L1 (baseline) | -0.28 | 0.61 | 0.85 | 1.049 | 0.045 |
| L2 | -0.00 | 0.84 | 1.05 | 1.365 | 0.083 |
| bone-weighted L1 x5 | -0.51 | 0.89 | 0.74 | 0.979 | 0.095 |
| bone-weighted L1 x20 | -0.65 | 1.01 | 0.71 | 0.976 | 0.128 |
| recalibrate (g=1.00) | -0.28 | 0.61 | 0.85 | 1.049 | 0.045 |

L2 is worse; bone-weighting buys a few % on bone MAE while degrading edge sharpness and soft tissue; recalibration's best gain was ~1.0 (no help, because the undershoot is blur+scatter, not a multiplicative bias). None beats the information ceiling.

## 4. Retrieval / template prior (the proposed idea), tested honestly

A retrieved + registered donor CT is modeled as an extra template channel with two independent quality axes mirroring reality: intensity fidelity (can the retrieval key find a donor with the right bone HU? bounded by the MR) and registration fidelity (residual bone-edge misalignment).

Construction choices, stated for honesty: (1) the `retrieved` donor height uses the same formula as the MR's own marker (independent noise), i.e. a second redundant readout of MR-accessible info, the intended model of "retrieval key = the MR", and why `retrieved` improves with alpha (variance reduction of an already-present signal, not new information). (2) `trueH_misreg` deliberately uses worse registration (jitter 2.5 vs 0.8), so it does not isolate one axis; it shows true-HU content survives bad registration. (3) `oracle` equals the target by construction (perfect soft + true bone HU/location); it is the unreachable upper bound, never evidence that retrieval works.

### Information-limited regime (alpha=0)

| variant | amp bias | amp scatter | edge sharp | bone MAE | soft MAE |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| none | -0.28 | 0.61 | 0.85 | 1.049 | 0.045 |
| retrieved | -0.14 | 0.57 | 0.84 | 0.931 | 0.034 |
| meanH_reg | -0.15 | 0.55 | 0.86 | 0.935 | 0.035 |
| trueH_misreg | -0.27 | 0.30 | 0.92 | 0.786 | 0.046 |
| oracle | +0.00 | 0.00 | 1.00 | 0.003 | 0.000 |

### Information-present regime (alpha=1)

| variant | amp bias | amp scatter | edge sharp | bone MAE | soft MAE |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| none | -0.32 | 0.52 | 0.84 | 0.999 | 0.047 |
| retrieved | -0.16 | 0.32 | 0.90 | 0.755 | 0.037 |
| meanH_reg | -0.18 | 0.45 | 0.88 | 0.883 | 0.038 |
| trueH_misreg | -0.28 | 0.31 | 0.94 | 0.778 | 0.047 |
| oracle | +0.00 | 0.00 | 1.00 | 0.004 | 0.001 |

Realistic retrieval (`retrieved` / `meanH_reg`) gives a modest bone-MAE gain by providing a sharp, located scaffold (helps shape/blur), but its intensity is bounded by what the MR key can access: at alpha=0 the amp scatter barely moves. The big wins (`trueH`, `oracle`) require knowing the bone HU, i.e. side-information the MR does not have. `oracle` (true HU + true location) is essentially perfect, confirming the problem is solvable iff you can supply the answer as a prior.

## 5. Generative (conditional diffusion): sharpness without accuracy

A conditional 1D DDPM samples CT|MR instead of regressing its mean.

### alpha=0 (information-limited)

| variant | amp bias | amp scatter | edge sharp | bone MAE | soft MAE |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| regressor (L1) | -0.28 | 0.61 | 0.85 | 1.049 | 0.045 |
| diffusion (1 sample) | -0.81 | 0.94 | 1.03 | 1.821 | 0.035 |
| diffusion (posterior mean) | -1.22 | 1.23 | 0.40 | 1.639 | 0.033 |

### alpha=1 (information-present)

| variant | amp bias | amp scatter | edge sharp | bone MAE | soft MAE |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| regressor (L1) | -0.32 | 0.52 | 0.84 | 0.999 | 0.047 |
| diffusion (1 sample) | -0.76 | 0.88 | 1.03 | 1.826 | 0.034 |
| diffusion (posterior mean) | -1.16 | 1.16 | 0.41 | 1.640 | 0.032 |

A single diffusion sample restores peak amplitude and edge sharpness to ~real-CT (it fixes both reported failure modes), but its per-voxel bone MAE is not lower than the regressor: the sample is realistic, not pixel-aligned. The posterior mean collapses back toward the regressor (sampling buys realism, not accuracy). At alpha=0 the sampled heights are plausible guesses from the prior, so per-voxel accuracy stays at the information ceiling.

Metric note: edge_sharp is a ratio of max local gradients (clipped at 2), so it credits gradient magnitude and a noisy prediction could in principle inflate it. Here the sharpness claim is also backed by the qualitative overlay (samples are visibly clean sharp bones, not noise) and the paired bone_mae (which penalizes the sample's positional error).

**Important caveat (real vs toy).** This toy is a pixel-space DDPM under ideal conditions, so its samples reach real-CT edge sharpness. On real data, report 11 found the existing generative baselines (MC-DDPM, cWDM, MAISI) still blur bone (edge sharpness ~0.79-0.82, no better than the U-Net's 0.81; the one model at 0.87 is koalAI, a regression baseline, not diffusion), because they operate through lossy latent/wavelet bottlenecks (the MAISI VAE skull bleed is the clearest case). So "a generative head restores bone sharpness" is demonstrated in principle here but is NOT delivered by current real models: realizing it needs a pixel-space or high-frequency-preserving design, not off-the-shelf latent diffusion.

## 6. Synthesis: one information ceiling

Sweeping alpha_info shows four behaviours. The regressor is blur-capped (flat ~1.0 in alpha): at jitter=1.5 its per-voxel bone error is dominated by edge blur, so more intensity information alone barely helps. Realistic retrieval improves with MR info (it can rescale its template) but plateaus far above the oracle. Only the oracle template (true side-information) reaches both low MAE and real-CT edges. The diffusion sample is highest on MAE (it optimizes realism, not MAE, and MAE rewards blur) yet leads on edge sharpness at every alpha. The intensity/blur split in one figure: per-voxel HU accuracy is information-capped, sharpness is not.

## 7. Real-data confirmation: the bone information floor

Key fact: any MR embedding is a deterministic function of the MR, so no MR-based predictor (U-Net, retrieval, diffusion) can carry more bone information than the MR itself. This ceiling is estimated model-free on real SynthRAD center-wise data (donors = train center-A subjects; queries = the U-Net's own OOD val set, center C for head&neck and center B for thorax). Baselines, all evaluated at the same bone voxels:

- **no-info prior**: knows the bone mask and predicts the population-mean bone HU, using zero MR. A privileged lower bound on "use no MR information".
- **retrieval (kNN, MR)**: k=16 nearest neighbors in local-MR-patch space (retrieval by MR appearance), prediction = neighbor-mean CT HU. A model-free MR predictor.
- **U-Net sCT**: the trained model, the strong learned MR predictor.
- **neighbor spread**: within-MR-neighborhood CT-HU std, an estimate of the irreducible floor (loose, since raw-patch kNN is a weak metric).

### Head & Neck (donors 12, queries 6)

| tissue | no-info prior | retrieval (kNN, MR) | U-Net sCT | neighbor spread (floor) | n |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| bone | 324 | 300 | 242 | 250 | 15000 |
| soft | 60 | 127 | 75 | 162 | 15000 |

### Thorax (donors 12, queries 6)

| tissue | no-info prior | retrieval (kNN, MR) | U-Net sCT | neighbor spread (floor) | n |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| bone | 155 | 183 | 162 | 142 | 15000 |
| soft | 62 | 87 | 41 | 100 | 15000 |

For bone, the U-Net is at the information floor: it improves only ~25% over the privileged no-MR prior on skull and on thorax fails to beat it (162 vs 155 HU, slightly worse), with retrieval-by-MR no better. For soft tissue the picture flips on thorax: the U-Net clearly beats the prior (41 vs 62 HU), the positive control showing the MR does carry soft-tissue information. This is the real-data analogue of the toy's alpha~0 (bone) vs alpha>0 (soft) regimes. Read the ceiling off the U-Net-vs-prior gap, not the raw-patch kNN: the kNN is a weak predictor (worse than the prior even for soft tissue), so it corroborates direction but its absolute MAE and the neighbor-spread are loose, not tight floors.

### Is this an OOD artifact? No, an in-distribution control says it is intrinsic.

The fairest objection: donors are center A and queries are a different center, so "U-Net fails to beat the prior" could be the U-Net being OOD rather than the MR being uninformative. Two defenses. (1) The prior eats the same center shift (it is the donor center-A bone mean scored on the query center), yet the MR-using U-Net still does not beat it, so whatever OOD penalty exists hits both and the MR adds nothing on top. (2) A direct in-distribution control, donors AND queries both center A (no domain shift):

| tissue | no-MR prior | retrieval (kNN, MR) | neighbor spread |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| bone | 266 | 243 | 179 |
| soft | 56 | 73 | 102 |

In-distribution, MR retrieval gets bone HU only ~9% below the no-MR prior (243 vs 266) with a large irreducible spread (179 HU). Bone HU is largely MR-unpredictable without any domain shift, so the information limit is intrinsic, not OOD. (Soft retrieval is worse than its prior here too, confirming the kNN is just a weak predictor; the soft information lives in the MR but this particular estimator does not extract it, which is why the learned U-Net, not the kNN, is the soft positive control.)

**Robustness caveats (honest limits of this real-data section).**
(a) Single seed, 12 donors / 6 query subjects per region; voxels within a subject are correlated so the effective n is ~subjects not voxels, and the ~25% / ~0% figures are point estimates without per-subject CIs. (b) The "neighbor spread" is a loose upper estimate of the floor (raw-patch L2 kNN is a weak metric), not a tight irreducible bound. (c) On head&neck the U-Net soft MAE (75) is itself worse than the prior (60), an OOD soft-tissue offset on center C; the clean soft positive control is thorax. None of these flips the bone verdict (corroborated by the in-distribution control, the toy mechanism, and report 11's six-model convergence), but the precise percentages should be read as indicative.

## 8. What to actually do

**Stop:** loss reweighting, bone-Dice tuning, removing the sigmoid cap, post-hoc HU gain. Proven not to move the per-voxel bone ceiling (here and in report 11's 6-model convergence).

**To raise the intensity ceiling (the hard half), inject information the MR lacks:**
- Bone-sensitive MR (UTE/ZTE/dual-echo) directly raises alpha; the cleanest fix where the sequence is available (SynthRAD is single T1/T2, so likely not, but worth confirming per cohort).
- Atlas / retrieval prior carrying population bone HU + shape as an extra input. Expect a modest per-voxel gain (toy: a sharp located template helps mostly shape/blur, intensity stays bounded), and beware misregistration. Worth it primarily for the shape/sharpness it contributes, not as an intensity cure.

**To fix the blur / realism half (cheaper, clinically real for DRR / bone visualization):**
- Generative head (diffusion or adversarial high-frequency term): in principle restores cortical edge sharpness even at the information ceiling. But it must be pixel-space or HF-preserving: report 11's existing latent/wavelet diffusion baselines do not (they blur bone like the U-Net). Best ROI if downstream cares about bone appearance (DRR) rather than exact per-voxel HU, and only with the right (non-lossy-bottleneck) architecture.

**Reframe the objective/eval** (connects to the decision-aware direction): bone HU is irreducibly uncertain from MR, so predict calibrated uncertainty on bone and evaluate on the downstream decision (dose / DRR), not pixel HU. The soft-tissue / PSNR story is separate and already near optimal; the cheap PSNR win there is the tight body mask (report 10), not modeling.
